1. Top 5 Things to Know Today

  1. New U.S. tariffs now cover almost all imports. President Trump replaced temporary levies with 10% to 12.5% tariffs on goods from 60 trading partners, a group accounting for roughly 99% of U.S. imports, and tied the action to forced-labor enforcement. The change raises costs and uncertainty across supply chains while trading partners challenge its legal and economic basis. AP, July 24
  2. The United States completed a 13th consecutive night of strikes on Iran as regional attacks continued. U.S. forces reported another round of strikes; explosive drones also targeted the Irbil area, where American troops are based, and warning sirens sounded in Bahrain. The widening cycle increases risks to civilians, deployed forces and energy shipping. AP, July 24
  3. The Education Department removed several disparate-impact civil-rights regulations. The provisions addressed policies that discriminate in effect even without stated discriminatory intent. Their removal narrows one federal enforcement route in education; civil-rights and education groups say students may lose protections, while the administration says enforcement should focus on intentional discrimination. Reuters, July 23 — link may be limited
  4. Twenty-five states and Washington, D.C., sued over election conditions attached to disaster grants. The states say DHS and FEMA cannot withhold 20% of more than $1 billion in emergency-preparedness funding unless they adopt federal preferences such as paper ballots and manual audits. The suit asks a Rhode Island federal court to separate disaster aid from election administration. Reuters, July 23 — link may be limited
  5. A Russian ballistic strike killed six people in Ukraine as Kyiv sought more Patriot defenses. The attack followed President Volodymyr Zelenskyy’s meeting with the U.S. company that makes Patriot systems; Ukrainian drones also struck warehouses in St. Petersburg. The developments underline Ukraine’s continuing air-defense shortage and the war’s cross-border reach. AP, July 24

2. Most Useful Development Today

A new cyclospora outbreak is under investigation

Federal health officials are investigating 72 illnesses in a cluster whose food source has not yet been identified; it is separate from the earlier Taylor Farms outbreak. Cyclospora can cause frequent or prolonged watery diarrhea, cramping, fatigue and weight loss. If symptoms are severe or persistent, contact a clinician and mention possible food exposure; careful handwashing and cooking food to at least 158°F can reduce risk, although washing produce may not remove the parasite completely. AP, July 24

Fausto reached Category 2 strength with 100 mph winds far east of Hawaii. Forecast impacts to the islands remain uncertain, but forecasters warned of dangerous surf and rip currents reaching Southern California first and potentially Hawaii next week. AP, July 24

Government withdrew subpoenas aimed at three New York Times reporters

The Justice Department withdrew demands for reporters’ communications after acknowledging legal errors in the Air Force One leak investigation. A federal judge sharply questioned how the subpoenas were approved, making the reversal significant for press-freedom safeguards and government process. AP, July 24

6. Science, Medicine & Public Health

FDA advisers narrowly backed easier access to unapproved compounded peptides

In several 8–6 votes with one abstention, an FDA advisory panel recommended treating BPC-157, TB-500 and KPV as acceptable for pharmacy compounding. The recommendation is not binding; FDA staff found little evidence of safety or effectiveness, including no human studies for TB-500, and the agency must still decide what action to take. AP, July 24

New Free Journal Articles

Item comprehension and content coverage of patient-reported outcome measures for long COVID: a qualitative study

Quality of Life Research · Online July 23, 2026 · Peer-reviewed qualitative study. Researchers conducted virtual cognitive-debriefing interviews with 30 English-speaking adults with Long COVID. Participants generally understood and considered the measures relevant, but wording, instructions and survey length created problems, and researchers observed cognitive burden. Why it matters: trials, clinical care and disability documentation depend on measures patients can complete accurately. Limitation: the small, English-speaking sample was recruited through a research firm; this study tests measurement, not a treatment. Free full text

Impact of an 18-month non-native language intervention on episodic memory and the underlying neural mechanisms in cognitively unimpaired older adults

Alzheimer’s & Dementia (Amsterdam) · Online July 23, 2026 · Secondary analysis of a randomized trial. Among 89 cognitively unimpaired adults age 65 or older—45 assigned to language training and 44 to no intervention—delayed recall was maintained in the intervention group while it declined in controls; memory change correlated with hippocampal perfusion. Why it matters: it tests a practical cognitive intervention and a possible neural correlate. Limitation: this modest secondary analysis does not show that language study prevents or treats Alzheimer’s disease, and replication is needed. Free full text at PubMed Central

Troponin T and Neurofilament Light Chain Levels as Complementary Biomarkers of Disease Accumulation and Aggressiveness in Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis

Annals of Clinical and Translational Neurology · Online July 23, 2026 · Retrospective observational study. Two German cohorts included 433 people in discovery and 185 in replication. Troponin T tracked more closely with accumulated disease stage, while serum neurofilament light better reflected aggressiveness, suggesting the biomarkers may capture different dimensions of ALS. Why it matters: better staging and prognosis could improve counseling and trial design for this Compassionate Allowances condition. Limitation: retrospective findings and incompletely replicated subgroup results require prospective longitudinal validation before routine prognostic use. Free full text

Also new: A systematic review of six randomized trials involving 961 participants found no strong evidence that adding versus switching antiseizure medication after a first drug fails produces better focal-epilepsy remission. The evidence was heterogeneous, mostly high risk of bias and based on trials from 1998–2012—useful chiefly for showing the need for modern comparative trials. Epileptic Disorders, online July 24 — free full text
