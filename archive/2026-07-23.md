## 2. Most Useful Development Today

### New review explains why functional-capacity exams should not stand alone

A new scoping review of **51 studies** found that functional capacity evaluations used in disability, insurance, return-to-work, and legal settings vary considerably in protocols and validation. The authors recommend standardized core testing plus job-specific modules, trained evaluators, clear reporting, and explicit attention to psychological, occupational, social, and environmental factors. For SSDI education, the useful point is not that the paper changes SSA rules—it does not—but that physical performance testing alone may incompletely represent real work capacity. [Free full article in Frontiers in Rehabilitation Sciences, July 22](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/rehabilitation-sciences/articles/10.3389/fresc.2026.1777330/full)

### New Free Journal Articles

- **[Temporal synchrony promotes motor recovery and ipsilesional cortical reorganization in BCI-based stroke rehabilitation](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12984-026-02100-9)** — *Journal of NeuroEngineering and Rehabilitation*, online July 22, 2026. **Evidence: peer-reviewed randomized controlled trial.** Seventy-seven people with subacute stroke were randomized to synchronous brain-computer-interface robotic training, asynchronous assistance, or sham training for three weeks; 68 were analyzed. The synchronous group had better upper-extremity motor and daily-function scores after treatment than both comparison groups. This is promising human rehabilitation evidence, but it is a short, single-center study, the analyzed sample was modest, and the neuroimaging findings were exploratory. [Free full text and PDF](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12984-026-02100-9)
- **[Functional capacity evaluations in medico-legal contexts: best practices, limitations, and areas for improvement](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/rehabilitation-sciences/articles/10.3389/fresc.2026.1777330/full)** — *Frontiers in Rehabilitation Sciences*, online July 22, 2026. **Evidence: peer-reviewed scoping review.** The authors synthesized 51 articles covering return-to-work, insurance, disability-assessment, and legal settings. They found inconsistent protocols, limited validation, and inadequate integration of psychosocial and job-context factors, and proposed more standardized and person-centered approaches. It directly informs disability and work-capacity research, but a scoping review maps heterogeneous evidence and cannot establish that one evaluation model produces better benefit decisions. [Free full text](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/rehabilitation-sciences/articles/10.3389/fresc.2026.1777330/full)
- **[Visual feedback increases expiratory muscle training effort in Parkinson’s disease](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12984-026-02104-5)** — *Journal of NeuroEngineering and Rehabilitation*, online July 22, 2026. **Evidence: peer-reviewed randomized crossover trial.** Twenty participants were randomized and 19 completed two expiratory-training conditions; real-time app feedback increased and stabilized abdominal-muscle activation during a single training session. The finding may help refine cough and swallowing rehabilitation, but it measured acute muscle activation rather than fewer aspiration events, better swallowing, or long-term clinical outcomes. [Free full text and PDF](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12984-026-02104-5)
