New Free Journal Articles

  • Communication makes intensive language therapy more efficient: a randomized-controlled trial in chronic post-stroke aphasia — Brain Communications, online accepted manuscript posted July 18, 2026. Evidence: randomized controlled human trial; open-access accepted manuscript. Forty-four adults with chronic post-stroke aphasia received two weeks of equally intensive therapy (12.5 hours/week): communication-focused Intensive Language Action Therapy or conventional speech-language therapy. The communication-focused group improved more on the language battery, and depression scores also favored that group. Why it matters: it suggests how therapy is delivered—not only its intensity—may improve rehabilitation efficiency and mood. Limits: small sample, short treatment period, and limited follow-up; results may not generalize beyond chronic aphasia.
  • Soluble high-molecular-weight amyloid-β species derived from amyloid-β-laden brains induce cerebral β-amyloidosis — Brain Communications, July 20, 2026. Evidence: peer-reviewed preclinical mouse and brain-tissue study; open access. Researchers found that soluble amyloid assemblies larger than 150 kDa from plaque-bearing mouse and human Alzheimer’s brains accelerated amyloid deposition when introduced into susceptible mice; smaller fractions did not. Why it matters: it narrows the candidate “seed” species that may drive spread and could inform future biomarkers or drug targets. Limits: this is mechanistic animal work, not a treatment trial; it does not show benefit or safety in people.
  • Duration of Lenalidomide Maintenance in Multiple Myeloma — New England Journal of Medicine, July 16, 2026. Evidence: recent phase 3 randomized human trial; free full article. In 516 people with standard-risk, newly diagnosed multiple myeloma who did not receive upfront autologous stem-cell transplantation, continuous maintenance until progression did not improve seven-year overall survival compared with stopping at two years and caused more toxicity. Why it matters: it directly informs the burden-versus-benefit question for long maintenance therapy. Limits: this recent fallback lies outside the 72-hour journal window; the findings may not apply to high-risk disease or transplant-treated patients, and treatment decisions remain individualized.
